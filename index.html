RoboMaster

At ZJU-UIUC Robotics Team, we build combat robots.

Being Control Group Lead and Project Manager in 2019, I led the development of embedded control systems and managed project timelines.

Meta Robotics of ZJU-UIUC Joint Institute, was found in 2018. I was one of the founding members. We target the RoboMaster competition and build our robots from scratch. Unfortunately, being a new team at a new campus without any experience and technology heritage, we didn't make it to the competition in 2018.

In 2019, I took the role of Control Group Lead. I led the development of embedded control programs and electric systems of all the robots, built from the ground up. We developed a comprehensive all-platform cross compile toolchain for STM32, which has been using by us ever since. We developed a system to support convenient parameter adjustment with Bluetooth and GUI. During the 2019 season, I contributed 70k+ lines of C++ code and 20+ documents.

Among the things we have built, I am most proud of the attitude and heading reference system (AHRS), in which a gyroscope, an accelerometer and a magnetometer are integrated to calculate the absolute angle of the targeting gimbal. Together with the motor control system, our robots were able to stabilize the gimbal while rotating the chassis to evade incoming bullets, which provided conspicuous advantages to us at the competition.

At the same time, I took the role of Project Manager in 2019. I was responsible for scheduling the overall development timeline, coordinating different groups of the team. I arranged the weekly stand-up meeting, when members exchanged updates and laid down upcoming schedule. Together with the team leader, we kept the whole team forward.

In RoboMaster 2019 International Regional Competition, as out debut, we won the second prize.

Since 2020, I worked as the Vision Group Lead of the team. I worked on a vision-assisted automatic aiming system, for which I have contributed 27k+ lines of C++ code.

JoyStick Module for NetEase Android Emulator

During Summer 2020, as Platform Engineer Intern at NetEase Game, I developed a driver module that maps various joysticks to a unified interface and configures keymap automatically using a predefined database, providing plug-and-play user experience for joystick users of the NetEase Android Emulator.

Read more >

There are various joysticks in the market. On the Windows platform, most joysticks use the DirectInput interface, which is flexible but lacks keymap standards. For example, the same key in DirectInput can map to key A for one joystick, but to key B for another.

When Microsoft rolled out their own joystick—the Xbox controllers, a new interface, XInput, was introduced. XInput is defined more clearly and easier to use, but only supports Xbox controllers.

On the other hands, as Nintendo Switch becomes popular, more and more users own Nintendo Switch Joy-Cons and want to use them in the NetEase Android Emulator. However, being a pair of two detached controllers, Joy-Cons are recognized as two DirectInput devices on Windows. To use the two pieces as one, either the program needs to support reading from two joysticks simultaneously, or they needs to be merged at the driver level. In addition, the advanced functions of Joy-Cons, such as the gyroscope and the haptic feedback, are not exposed through DirectInput.

We intended to provide smooth user experience for joystick users of the NetEase Android Emulator by introducing an adapter module. When a user plugs a joystick, the module looks up the joystick in a database and deploys the common keymap automatically. The user can also manually configure the keymap through an intuitive GUI. For Joy-Cons, two pieces are merged automatically (in fact, the module support merging arbitrary number of joysticks). Gyroscope and haptic feedback are supported. Users can map the gyroscope to axes or buttons, turning games into interactive style.

Technically, the module maps various joystick backends (DirectInput, XInput, and HID) to the unified XInput interface, minimizing changes in the Emulator main program. Joy-Cons are controlled through HID interface rather than DirectInput to leverage the unexposed functions. The keymap configuration GUI is implemented with Qt.

KLC3

KLEE on LC‑3. A symbolic execution engine for LC‑3 (an educational assembly) based on LLVM KLEE for automatic bug detection and test case generation. KLC3 was used to provide automatic end-to-end feedback to 100+ sophomore students for their LC‑3 assignments in Fall 2020. Our work was published at ASE 2021.

Read more >

My Presentation at ASE 2021

Learning to program is difficult. As with all topics, students learn more quickly when given immediate feedback tailored to their efforts. However, while university staff—instructors and teaching assistants—are capable of providing such feedback, staff are not available 24/7 and lack the time needed to provide individual attention to each student's programs.

Automating feedback has been an important topic for years. A simple and widely adopted approach is to use test-case-based systems. However, these systems typically fail to provide feedback tailored to each student's effort.

To address the need of rapid and tailored feedback, we develop a system for end-to-end automatic feedback. Specifically, we target LC‑3, a RISC-like educational assembly used in the courses of UIUC and other universities. At the heart of the system is KLC3, a concolic (concrete + symbolic) execution engine for LC‑3 built on top of LLVM KLEE.

At high-level, symbolic execution is a technique that treats inputs as symbols rather than concrete values, operates them the same way as the program executes, and checks the result symbolically. Intuitively, it executes all the possible values concurrently. It provides additional power to the feedback system, not only to perform more thorough test, but also to generate test cases tailored to the students' programs, which can be released to the students without undermining the power of the system.

We also built tools around KLC3 into the system to cover the whole workflow of editing, testing, and debugging when students program. At the frontend, we develop a VSCode extension that provides real-time feedback to students while they are editing their code, leveraging static analysis to provide warnings of possible bugs or poor styles. At the backend, we develop a fully fledged in-browser LC‑3 simulator with WebAssembly, freeing students from compiling the Unix LC‑3 tools locally.

We actually deployed the system to provide feedback for the assembly programming assignments in a class of over 100 students in Fall 2020. Students wrote a median of around 700 lines of assembly for these assignments, making heavy use of our tools to understand and eliminate their bugs. Anonymous student feedback on the tools was uniformly positive. Our work was published and presented at ASE 2021, the 36th IEEE/ACM International Conference on Automated Software Engineering.

Concolic execution is powerful, but the power doesn't come for free. It can be time-consuming. A lot of effort has been settled to enable KLC3 to provide feedback in time after students submit their code. Those work also form parts of my senior thesis of the Bachelor's degree.

Wireless Charging Table with Auto Alignment

As our Bachelor's senior design project, my teammates and I built a wireless charging table that automatically aligns the charging coils with the devices, combining electrical engineering, computer vision, and mechanical system. We won the most Interdisciplinary Project Award at the ZJU-UIUC Institute.

Read more >

Demonstration of the Table Charging Multiple Cellphones

Nowadays, more and more mobile devices support wireless charging. Among them, the Qi standard is the most widely adopted wireless charging protocol. Qi hotspots can be found in many public places, such as cafeterias and airports. However, the Qi protocol requires proper alignment between the charging coil and the device being charged (by the time we developed this project, Apple MagSafe for iPhones is not yet available).

Our goal is to provide seamless wireless charging experience at Qi hotspots like cafeterias, where customers sit around tables. We designed a smart table that automatically aligns wireless charging coils with users' devices, which are detected with computer vision. The coils are moved by a mechanical system like 3D printers. Up to 3 devices can be charged concurrently. Phone cases with common thickness are allowed. The system is able to detect if a device on the table supports wireless charging with a few trials. If not, the device is ignored.

We designed and implemented the table. Our team won the most Interdisciplinary Project Award of ECE445 Senior Design at the ZJU-UIUC Joint Institute in 2021.

Our Team at ECE391 Design Competition

During Fall 2019, as a part of the UIUC ECE391 course, my teammates and I designed and implemented AuroraOS, an x86 operating system. The system was emulated in QEMU.

We implemented the basic kernel features required by the course, including memory management, real-time clock, interrupts, system calls, user program loader, a read-only file system, terminal, and a round-robin scheduler.

In additions to these basic features, we implemented a handful of extra functions. We implemented the 1024x768 16-bit high color SVGA driver with hardware acceleration and alpha blending. On top of it, we built an interactive graphic user interface to support multiple terminals as well as mouse control. We also implemented an advanced scheduler with wait lists (similar to the one in Linux) and CMOS clock. To ease the development and debug work, we also employed a cross compile toolchain from macOS to x86.

ECE391 hosted an OS design competition every semester. In Fall 2019, our team got the third prize at the competition.

Boxhead Game on FPGA

As the UIUC ECE385 design project, my teammate and I developed the Boxhead video game on the Altera DE2-115. We wrote the SRAM controller and the hardware graphic engine in SystemVerilog, and the game logic in C.

Read more >

As the final design project of UIUC ECE411 Computer Organization and Design in Fall 2020, my teammates and I designed and simulates a RV32I processor in SystemVerilog. The processor has 5-stage pipeline with data forwarding and hazard detection. We implemented the Tournament Branch Predictor, combining both local and global branch histories. As for caches, we implemented a parameterized cache module with the pseudo LRU replacement policy, from which L1/L2 I-/D-caches are instantiated. In addition, we also implemented a next-line prefetcher.
